## Share sizes — the basics

When a syndicator prepares a horse for syndication, they divide ownership into a fixed number of shares. A horse with 20 shares of 5% each has 20 owners who each contribute and receive proportionally. A horse split into 100 shares of 1% each has a larger ownership group with smaller individual commitments. The total always adds to 100%.

The syndicator typically retains a small percentage (often 5–10%) and offers the remainder to the public through a PDS. The unsold shares remain the syndicator's cost centre — which is one reason syndicators are motivated to find a great trainer and put the horse in position to win.

## What your share percentage means in practice

Your share percentage determines three things:

**Your upfront cost.** If the total purchase price of the syndicate is $100,000 and you own 5%, you pay $5,000 upfront (plus GST, as applicable).

**Your weekly training cost.** If the trainer charges $3,500 per week for the horse and you own 5%, you contribute $175 per week. Syndicators usually invoice monthly — so budget approximately $750 per month at this rate.

**Your prizemoney entitlement.** When the horse wins $50,000 in prizemoney, the trainer's percentage (typically 10%) is deducted, leaving $45,000. Your 5% share means you receive $2,250. Prizemoney is generally paid through the syndicator after the racing authority processes it.

## Choosing the right share size

For a first-time owner, a 2.5% to 5% share is a sensible starting point. It gives you full owner privileges — mounting yard access, owners room entry, stable visits — at a cost that is meaningful but not ruinous if the horse doesn't reach the track.

Larger shares (10% and above) make sense if you want a more significant stake in a horse you believe in strongly, or if you want a more active relationship with the syndicator and trainer. Some syndicators offer preferential communication to larger shareholders, though this varies.

Micro-shares (1% or less) reduce your financial exposure to a minimum but also reduce prizemoney entitlements to the point where a win might return less than $1,000. They are great for the experience and the raceday access; less relevant as a cost-sharing mechanism.

## Fractional shares and minimum investments

Some syndicators offer fractional shares — for example, 2.5% instead of a round number. This flexibility allows the syndicator to fill the syndicate exactly to 100% while accommodating investors at different price points. When reviewing a PDS, check what fractions are on offer and whether the syndicate is fully subscribed — a horse that hasn't been fully syndicated may not be ready to go to the trainer.

## A note on equity vs. lease arrangements

Everything described above relates to ownership syndicates, where you buy a percentage of the horse itself. Some trainers offer racing syndicates or racing leases, where the horse is leased from its owner for a racing season, and participants buy a share of the lease rather than the horse. The cost structure is similar, but you do not own any equity in the horse at the end of the term. Always check the PDS carefully to understand whether you are buying ownership or a lease.

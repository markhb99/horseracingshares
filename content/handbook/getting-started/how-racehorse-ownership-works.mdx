## What racehorse ownership actually means

In Australia, owning a racehorse does not mean you go out and buy a horse, stable it in your backyard, and send it to the track on Saturdays. For the overwhelming majority of owners, it means purchasing a percentage share in a horse that is trained by a professional trainer, managed by a licensed syndicator, and raced under Australian rules of racing administered by Racing Australia and the relevant state principal racing authority.

When you buy a share, you are buying a proportional interest in the horse's racing career. If you own 5%, you are entitled to 5% of any prizemoney the horse earns, after the trainer's and jockey's fees are deducted. You also contribute 5% of the ongoing weekly training costs, race day expenses, insurance, and veterinary fees. The arrangement is governed by a syndicate deed — a legal document that spells out exactly what you own and what you're obligated to pay.

## Step 1 — Find a listing and read the PDS

Before anything else, read the Product Disclosure Statement. Every legally marketed racehorse syndicate in Australia must be offered under an AFSL (Australian Financial Services Licence) held by the syndicator, and every offer must be accompanied by a PDS that discloses the horse's purchase price, the fee structure, the syndicate structure, and the risks. Reading the PDS is not a formality — it is the document that protects you if something goes wrong.

## Step 2 — Choose your share size

Share sizes typically range from 1% to 25%, though some syndicators offer slices as small as 0.5%. A smaller share means lower upfront cost and lower ongoing liability, but also a smaller slice of any prizemoney. Most first-time owners start with a 2.5% to 5% share to test the experience without overcommitting financially.

## Step 3 — Register as an owner with Racing Australia

Every person or entity that owns a share in a racehorse must be registered with Racing Australia and the relevant state authority. Registration involves a standard identity check and a modest annual fee. Your syndicator will guide you through the process — see our [step-by-step Racing Australia registration guide](/handbook/getting-started/racing-australia-registration) for full details.

## Step 4 — Sign the syndicate deed

Once you have read the PDS, chosen your share, and completed owner registration, you will be asked to sign the syndicate deed. This is the binding contract. Make sure you understand the clauses around exit conditions, what happens if the horse is injured or retired early, and how decisions about the horse are made (usually by the syndicator in consultation with the trainer).

## Step 5 — Pay and participate

After signing, you pay the purchase price for your share. From that point, you receive regular stable updates from the trainer, invitations to trackwork mornings, raceday owner privileges (mounting yard access, owners room, complimentary tickets), and — hopefully — the occasional slice of prizemoney.

## What "ownership" does not include

Racehorse ownership in Australia is a leisure and sporting activity, not a financial strategy. The vast majority of horses do not earn enough in prizemoney to cover their costs. You should enter ownership expecting to spend money for the experience, the community, and the genuine thrill of watching a horse you part-own competing on a big race day. Any prizemoney earned is a bonus, not a return on investment.

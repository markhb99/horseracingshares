## The basics

A racehorse syndicate is a group of people who collectively own a horse. Instead of one person bearing the full cost of buying, training, and racing a thoroughbred, the costs and the experience are shared across multiple owners — anywhere from two people to, in some cases, fifty or more.

In Australia, syndicates must be structured and marketed according to the Corporations Act 2001 and the rules set by ASIC. Any public offer of a racehorse syndicate share is a regulated financial product, which means the syndicator must hold an AFSL (or be an authorised representative of an AFSL holder) and must issue a Product Disclosure Statement for each horse.

## The key parties in a syndicate

**The syndicator** is the licensed entity that puts the syndicate together. They find the horse, negotiate the purchase price, arrange the trainer, manage the administrative and legal paperwork (including the PDS and syndicate deed), collect contributions from owners, and distribute prizemoney. The syndicator typically charges a management fee and may take a percentage of prizemoney.

**The trainer** is the licensed professional who prepares the horse for racing. They are not usually a party to the syndicate structure — they have a separate training agreement with the syndicator. The trainer charges a weekly training fee (which flows through to owners as part of the ongoing costs), plus fees for race day expenses, farrier, physiotherapy, and related costs.

**The syndicate members** — you — are the beneficial owners of shares. Your rights and obligations are defined in the syndicate deed: what percentage you own, what you pay, how long the syndicate is expected to run, and what happens at the end (sale of the horse, retirement, etc.).

## How costs and prizemoney flow

Every week, the syndicator collects training fees from all members in proportion to their share. If weekly training costs are $3,000 for the horse and you own 5%, you pay $150 per week. On top of the weekly training fee, there are periodic race day costs: the race nomination fee, acceptance, jockey fee, and transport. When the horse wins prizemoney, Racing Australia distributes it to the racing club, which pays the trainer's percentage and then transfers the balance to the syndicator, who distributes it to members proportionally.

## What to check before joining a syndicate

Before committing to any syndicate, confirm:

1. **AFSL number** — the syndicator must have one or be an authorised representative. Check ASIC's register at moneysmart.gov.au.
2. **PDS** — read it entirely. The PDS must disclose all fees, the purchase price of the horse, the management structure, the risks, and the exit conditions.
3. **Trainer track record** — look up the trainer on Racing Australia's website. How many winners did they prepare last season? What is their strike rate?
4. **Horse breeding and veterinary history** — good syndicators provide x-rays and endoscope ("scope") results. Check for pre-existing conditions that might limit the horse's racing life.
5. **Subscription period** — how long do you have to decide? A legitimate syndicator does not rush you.

A syndicate is a long-term commitment, often two to four years. Take your time, ask questions, and never sign a deed until you have read and understood the PDS.

Your horse is declared to race. Your owner's badge has arrived. You've booked your spot in the car park and ironed your shirt. Here is everything you need to know to make the most of your first raceday as an owner.

## Before you go: preparation

**Confirm the declaration.** Horses are declared to race two days before most metropolitan and provincial races (and the day before country races in some states). Your syndicator will notify you when your horse is declared, but you can also check on Racing Australia's website or the relevant state authority's app.

**Arrange your badge.** Your owner's badge entitles you to enter the owners and trainers enclosure, the mounting yard, and typically the members' area for the race day. Badges are usually arranged through the syndicator, who notifies the racing club of owner details. Collect your badge at the owners badge collection point at the track, or in some cases they are sent to you in advance.

**Know what to wear.** The dress code at Australian racecourses varies by venue and enclosure. For the owners room and mounting yard, smart casual is the minimum — no thongs, singlets, or activewear. For major metropolitan races, a jacket for men and smart dress for women is the expectation. Check the specific venue's dress code policy.

**Allow plenty of time.** There is more to see on raceday than the race itself. Arrive at least 90 minutes before your horse's scheduled race time.

## At the track: the mounting yard

The mounting yard is where your horse is brought before a race and where the trainer, jockey, and syndicator meet for the final instructions. As an owner, you are allowed in the mounting yard for your horse's race. This is one of the great privileges of ownership.

When your horse arrives in the mounting yard, take the time to look at it. Is it calm? Sweating? Excited? Your trainer will be happy to talk you through the horse's condition and the race plan. This is usually a focused 5–10 minutes — the trainer is preparing the jockey, not there for an extended conversation, but they will acknowledge you and give you the basics.

When the jockey is legged up and the horses parade past the mounting yard rail, this is your moment. The horse you own a share of is right in front of you, about to compete.

## Watching the race

Find your spot at the winning post or a good vantage point well before your race comes up. Metropolitan racecourses have large screens that show the field and replays. The race caller will announce the horses by name.

For most first-time owners, the race passes in about 90 seconds of intense excitement. Every length your horse gains or loses is immediately felt. This is why people get addicted to ownership.

## After the race: the winner's stall and beyond

If your horse wins, you are entitled to enter the winner's stall for the presentation — the moment when the trainer, jockey, and owners are photographed together beside the horse. Accept the trophy if offered. Smile. This is one of Australian racing's great traditions.

Win or lose, head to the owners room or lounge after the race. This is where connections gather, drinks are poured, and the race is replayed and discussed. Even if your horse finishes last, the post-race debrief with the trainer is informative and usually entertaining.

## Common first-raceday mistakes to avoid

- **Leaving too early.** Even if your horse runs poorly, stay for the debrief. You'll learn something.
- **Not introducing yourself to the trainer.** They want to meet you. Go and say hello.
- **Missing the mounting yard.** It's the best part of ownership. Don't skip it to find a beer.
- **Not bringing a camera.** You will want photos. The mounting yard moment is worth capturing.
- **Betting beyond your comfort.** You don't need to bet. Many owners don't. If you do, be sensible.

## After raceday: the follow-up

After the event, expect an update from your syndicator within a day or two summarising the race, any immediate veterinary observations, and the plan going forward. This is the rhythm of ownership: preparation, raceday, debrief, and on to the next one.

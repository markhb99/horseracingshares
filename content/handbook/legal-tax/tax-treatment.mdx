## Is racehorse ownership a business or a hobby?

The ATO's treatment of your racehorse ownership depends heavily on whether you are carrying on a business or a hobby. For most individual syndicate members with a small percentage share in one or two horses, the ATO is likely to view the activity as a hobby. This has important implications:

- **Hobby:** prizemoney is not assessable income, and costs are not deductible. You cannot claim training fees or race day costs against your taxable income.
- **Business:** prizemoney is assessable income, and legitimate costs (training fees, race day expenses, registration fees) are deductible against that income. Losses from the business may or may not be deductible in the current year, depending on the non-commercial loss rules.

The distinction is not simply a matter of choice — the ATO applies an objective test based on factors like the commercial intent of the activity, whether it is carried on in a businesslike manner, and whether there is a realistic prospect of generating profit.

## The "profit from racing" test

For a racehorse activity to be treated as a business, the ATO generally wants to see:

1. A genuine intention and reasonable expectation of making profit
2. Activities carried out in a businesslike manner (records, budgets, business planning)
3. A significant level of activity

A single 5% share in one syndicate, purchased primarily for the experience, is very unlikely to satisfy the business test. A professional syndicator running multiple horses is almost certainly carrying on a business.

## GST considerations

If you are registered for GST and the activity is part of a GST enterprise, you may be able to claim input tax credits on the GST component of training fees and other costs. However, prizemoney distributed to owners is not subject to GST (it is not a supply). GST registration for a small ownership stake is unusual and the compliance cost typically exceeds any benefit. Get specific advice from your accountant.

## Record keeping

Regardless of your tax position, keep detailed records:

- All invoices from the syndicator (training fees, race day costs, vet bills)
- All prizemoney receipts
- Owner registration fees
- Any incidental costs (travel to trackwork or raceday, if relevant)

Records should be kept for at least five years. Your syndicator should provide an annual statement of fees charged and prizemoney distributed — if they don't, ask for one.

## Capital gains

If the syndicate sells the horse at the end of its racing career, the proceeds are distributed to owners in proportion to their share. Whether a capital gain arises depends on how the asset is classified. For a hobby, there is generally no CGT event (the horse is a personal use asset). For a business, the sale proceeds would be ordinary income, not a capital gain.

## What to do

Before joining a syndicate, ask your accountant how the arrangement will be treated in your specific circumstances. The answer depends on your overall tax position, whether you have other racing interests, and the structure of the syndicate. Don't assume the tax treatment — get advice before you sign.

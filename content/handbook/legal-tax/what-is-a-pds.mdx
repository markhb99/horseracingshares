## What is a PDS and why does it exist?

A Product Disclosure Statement is a legal document that must be issued before any offer of a regulated financial product to retail investors in Australia. Under the Corporations Act 2001, a racehorse syndicate share is a managed investment scheme and therefore a financial product. Every syndicator must hold an AFSL (Australian Financial Services Licence) and must provide a PDS for each horse offered to the public.

The PDS exists to protect you. Before investing in anything, you have the right to know exactly what you are buying, what it costs, who is managing your money, and what the risks are. A PDS delivers all of this in a standardised format.

## What must a PDS include?

By law, a PDS for a racehorse syndicate must disclose:

**The horse:** purchase price, breed, age, sex, and veterinary history. Any known health issues must be disclosed.

**The syndicate structure:** total shares available, the syndicator's retained percentage, and any restrictions on transferring your share.

**All fees:** the syndicator's management fee, the trainer's fee structure, race day cost estimates, and any other charges. There must be no hidden fees.

**The syndicator's AFSL details:** the AFSL number and the name of the authorised representative (if applicable). You can verify this on ASIC's public register.

**Risks:** a frank assessment of the risks, including the horse being injured, the syndicate making a loss, and the lack of a liquid secondary market for shares.

**Dispute resolution:** the syndicator must nominate an ASIC-approved external dispute resolution scheme that you can use if things go wrong.

## How to read a PDS

A racehorse PDS is typically 20–50 pages. Here is what to focus on:

1. **Fees page:** add up all the disclosed fees. What is the total cost per 1% share per year, assuming average race frequency?
2. **Horse history section:** has the horse had any vet issues? Has it trialled? What did it sell for at auction?
3. **Syndicator background:** how long have they been operating? How many horses have they syndicated? What is their track record?
4. **Exit conditions:** what happens if you want to sell your share before the syndicate ends? Is there a mechanism for this? What happens if the horse is injured and retired early?
5. **Insurance:** is the horse insured? For what amount? What events trigger the policy?

## Red flags to watch for

- A PDS that does not clearly disclose the syndicator's AFSL number
- Fees that are buried in footnotes or described in vague terms
- No disclosure of veterinary history
- An exit condition that effectively locks you in for the full term with no recourse
- Returns presented as likely or certain (this would breach financial product marketing rules)

Owning a racehorse share is one of the most exciting things you can do in Australian sport — but it's not cheap, and the upfront purchase price is just the beginning. This guide breaks down every cost you should plan for before signing a syndicate deed.

## The upfront purchase price

The biggest single cost is the purchase of your share. For a horse bought at a yearling sale, total syndicate prices typically range from $60,000 for a modest country-bred prospect to $500,000 or more for a colt by a fashionable stallion. A 5% share in a $100,000 syndicate costs $5,000; the same percentage in a $500,000 syndicate costs $25,000.

The purchase price is disclosed in the PDS. It should include GST where applicable, and the PDS must separately disclose the syndicator's fee or margin — so you can see what the horse actually cost versus what you're being charged.

## Weekly training fees

After purchase, the biggest ongoing cost is the weekly training fee. Trainers typically charge between $80 and $130 per day per horse, which translates to $560 to $910 per week. On a 5% share, your weekly contribution is approximately $28 to $45. Over 52 weeks, that's $1,456 to $2,340 per year for your share.

Training fees cover the trainer's staff wages, feed, farrier, routine veterinary checks, and the cost of the stable. They do not cover race day costs or extraordinary vet bills.

## Race day costs

Every time the horse races, there are additional costs: the race nomination fee (typically $200–$500 for a metropolitan race), the acceptance fee, transport to the track ($100–$400 depending on distance), and the jockey's fee (approximately $300–$400 for metro, less for country). On a 5% share, your portion of a typical metropolitan race day might be $50–$80.

Horses typically race between 6 and 15 times per year if healthy. Budget for 10 race starts per year as a planning figure — but bear in mind that horses can be injured, withdrawn, or scratched. Some horses race far less than planned.

## Veterinary costs

Routine vet costs are included in the weekly training fee. Extraordinary costs — including surgery, prolonged rest and rehabilitation, or specialist scopes — are charged separately and can be significant. A surgery for a bone chip, for example, can cost $8,000–$15,000 for the horse, meaning $400–$750 for a 5% share. The PDS should disclose how extraordinary vet costs are handled and whether there is an insurance policy in place.

## Insurance

A quality syndicator will insure the horse against death, permanent disablement, and sometimes loss of use. Insurance premiums are typically charged back to owners as part of the ongoing costs. Check the PDS for the coverage levels and what happens if the horse is retired due to injury before the syndicate term ends.

## What is not included

The costs above relate specifically to the horse's racing career. They do not include:

- **Your travel costs** to attend trackwork, track trials, or race days
- **The Racing Australia owner registration fee** (approximately $66–$80 per year depending on your state)
- **Any syndicate management or communication platform fees** some syndicators charge

## The three-year picture

Use our [cost calculator](/handbook/the-numbers) to model your specific scenario. As a rough guide, a 5% share in a $100,000 syndicate with average costs might cost approximately:

- **Upfront:** $5,000
- **Year 1 ongoing (training + race days):** $3,500–$5,000
- **Year 2:** $3,500–$5,000
- **Year 3:** $3,500–$5,000

**Total 3-year cost (before prizemoney): approximately $15,500–$20,000 for a 5% share.**

Prizemoney can offset some of these costs, but should not be planned on. Racing is unpredictable. Budget for the full cost and treat any prizemoney as a welcome bonus.

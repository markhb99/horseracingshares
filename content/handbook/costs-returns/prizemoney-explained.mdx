## Where prizemoney comes from

Prizemoney in Australian thoroughbred racing is funded by a combination of wagering revenue (TAB and corporate bookmakers), minimum guarantee schemes run by the state racing authorities, and in some cases sponsorship from race naming rights partners.

The principal racing authority for each state — Racing Victoria, Racing NSW, Racing Queensland, and so on — sets the prizemoney schedule for each race category. A Benchmark 64 race at a provincial track might carry $25,000 in prizemoney; a Group 1 race in Melbourne or Sydney might carry $5,000,000. The racing club where the race is run distributes the prizemoney to connections after the event.

## The prizemoney split

Prizemoney is not all yours — several parties take a percentage before you see a cent.

**First place** typically receives 60–65% of the total prizemoney. Places 2 through 8 (depending on the race and field size) share the remainder. In a $25,000 race:
- 1st: $15,000 (60%)
- 2nd: $5,500
- 3rd: $2,500
- 4th–8th: share of the remainder

**The trainer** takes 10% of the connections' share of prizemoney (the industry standard, though this can vary — check the syndicate deed).

**The jockey** takes 5% of the connections' share, plus their basic riding fee.

So on a $15,000 first-place payment, after trainer (10%) and jockey (5%) deductions, owners receive approximately $12,750. On a 5% share, your share is $637.50.

## How prizemoney reaches your account

After a race, the racing club typically takes 3–7 business days to process and pay out prizemoney. The payment goes to the syndicator, who then distributes it to individual owners according to their share percentage. Syndicators usually process prizemoney distributions within a further 7–14 days, meaning you might receive your payment 2–3 weeks after race day.

Some syndicators apply prizemoney against outstanding training fee invoices before distributing the balance. Check the syndicate deed for the distribution policy.

## Bonus scheme prizemoney

On top of standard prizemoney, horses nominated to bonus schemes like [BOBS](/handbook/bonus-schemes/bobs-explained), [VOBIS](/handbook/bonus-schemes/vobis-explained), [QTIS](/handbook/bonus-schemes/qtis-explained), or [MMRS](/handbook/bonus-schemes/mmrs-explained) can earn additional bonus payments when they win or place in eligible races. These bonuses can significantly increase the total payment — a BOBS-nominated horse winning a country NSW race might earn $50,000 in bonus money on top of the standard race prizemoney.

Bonus prizemoney follows the same distribution chain as standard prizemoney: through the racing authority, to the syndicator, to owners.

## A note on tax treatment

Prizemoney received by owners is assessable income in Australia. The ATO's position on racing-related income and deductions is nuanced — see our [tax treatment guide](/handbook/legal-tax/tax-treatment) for details. You should keep records of all prizemoney received and all costs paid, and discuss the net position with a tax adviser.

## What prizemoney is not

Prizemoney is not a financial return in the sense of dividends or interest. It is income from a sporting activity. Most racehorses earn less in prizemoney over their career than their owners spend on training fees and race day costs combined. Ownership should be approached as a sporting and lifestyle experience; prizemoney is a welcome bonus, not the primary reason to participate.

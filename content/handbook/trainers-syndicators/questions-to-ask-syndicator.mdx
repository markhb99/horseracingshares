Before you sign a syndicate deed and hand over your money, a face-to-face or phone conversation with the syndicator is worthwhile. Here are the ten most important questions to ask.

## 1. Can I see your AFSL number and verify it?

Any syndicator offering racehorse syndicate shares to the public in Australia must hold an AFSL or be an authorised representative of an AFSL holder. The AFSL number must be on every piece of marketing material and on the PDS. Before anything else, ask for it, write it down, and verify it on ASIC's online register at moneysmart.gov.au. This takes two minutes and is non-negotiable.

## 2. Where does the PDS disclose your fees and margin?

The syndicator earns money somewhere — either through a margin on the horse's purchase price, a management fee, a percentage of prizemoney, or some combination. Ask them to take you through exactly where their revenue comes from. This is fully disclosed in the PDS, but having them explain it in plain English gives you a sense of how transparent they are.

## 3. How long have you been syndicating horses?

Experience matters. A syndicator with ten years of operation has dealt with injuries, difficult trainers, horses that never reached the track, and syndicate members who stopped paying their fees. Ask for their track record — total horses syndicated, approximate number that reached the track, any notable performers.

## 4. What happens if the horse is injured before it races?

This is the scenario that catches first-time owners by surprise. Ask specifically: if the horse is injured before its first race start, what happens to my upfront payment? What insurance is in place? Is there any refund mechanism? The answer will reveal how the syndicator handles difficult situations.

## 5. Can I exit my share before the syndicate ends?

There is no guaranteed secondary market for racehorse syndicate shares. Ask the syndicator whether they have ever facilitated the sale of a syndicate member's share to a new buyer. What is the process? Some syndicators actively maintain a waiting list of buyers; others offer no exit mechanism at all.

## 6. How do you communicate with owners?

Ask to see an example of a stable update or owner newsletter. Good syndicators communicate regularly, honestly, and with genuine information about the horse's progress — not just marketing material. If they cannot provide an example, that is a yellow flag.

## 7. Who is the trainer and why did you choose them?

The syndicator's answer here will tell you a lot about how they approach horse selection and placement. If they have a long relationship with the trainer and can articulate why this trainer suits this horse, that is reassuring. If the answer is vague or generic, probe further.

## 8. What is the expected subscription timeline?

A well-prepared syndication fills quickly. Ask how close to fully subscribed they are, and what happens if the syndicate doesn't fill completely before the horse needs to go to the trainer. Some syndicators absorb the difference themselves; others may delay training until the syndicate is full.

## 9. Have you ever had an ASIC compliance matter or owner complaint resolved by an external dispute resolution body?

Most reputable syndicators have clean records. Asking the question directly is worthwhile — a reluctance to answer is itself informative.

## 10. How do I contact you if something goes wrong?

Get a direct phone number, not just an email. Know who your primary contact is and how quickly they respond. Horses can have emergencies at 6am on a Saturday. A good syndicator is accessible and communicative in difficult moments.

---

A strong syndicator will welcome all of these questions. If any question is deflected or answered defensively, take that seriously. You are about to enter a multi-year financial relationship — the quality of the syndicator matters as much as the quality of the horse.

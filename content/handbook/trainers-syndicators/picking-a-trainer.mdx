When you buy a share in a racehorse, you are not just buying the horse — you are buying into the trainer's program, expertise, and track record. The trainer is the person who will spend more time with your horse than anyone else. Their judgment will determine where it races, when it races, and how it is prepared. Choosing wisely matters.

Here are twelve questions to ask before committing.

## 1. What is your winners-to-starters ratio?

The industry benchmark for a competent trainer in Australia is roughly 10–15% wins from starts. Elite metropolitan trainers often run higher. A trainer below 8% year after year may be taking on too many horses for their staff capacity, or may not have the skill to place horses correctly. Check the trainer's record on the Racing Australia website.

## 2. How many horses do you have in work?

Bigger is not always better. A trainer with 80 horses and two staff is probably not giving each horse the attention it deserves. A trainer with 25 horses and five dedicated staff might be better positioned to manage your horse individually. Ask about the staff-to-horse ratio.

## 3. Where do you race your horses — metro, provincial, or country?

Some trainers specialise in country racing with a high strike rate in lower-grade races. Others aim exclusively at metropolitan races. Neither is inherently better — it depends on the quality of the horse. But make sure the trainer's typical program aligns with the grade of horse being offered. A metropolitan trainer who rarely races country will struggle to take advantage of BOBS or VOBIS bonuses.

## 4. What is your relationship with the syndicator?

A long-term, trust-based relationship between trainer and syndicator produces better outcomes than a one-off arrangement. Ask how long they have worked together and how many horses they have trained together.

## 5. How do you communicate with owners?

The best trainers communicate proactively. What is the standard cadence — weekly updates? Stable visits? A dedicated owner portal or app? Find out what you'll actually receive in terms of information.

## 6. Can I visit the stable and watch trackwork?

Any legitimate trainer should welcome owners to visit the stable and watch morning trackwork. If a trainer is reluctant to allow visits, that is unusual and worth probing.

## 7. What is your approach to managing injuries?

Injuries are inevitable in thoroughbred racing. How does the trainer approach a horse that shows physical stress? Are they conservative (rest first) or aggressive (push through it)? Conservative management generally leads to longer racing careers.

## 8. Who is your principal jockey or jockey agent?

The trainer's relationship with their jockey or jockey bookings agent affects what riders your horse gets. A trainer with strong connections to good riders can be a meaningful advantage in big races.

## 9. What happens if the horse doesn't reach the track?

Some horses — perhaps 15–25% of all horses in training — never make it to the racecourse due to injury or ability limitations. If that happens, what is the trainer's approach? Do they recommend sale, retirement to a new career, or persistent investment? Understand this before you commit.

## 10. Do you take a percentage of prizemoney?

The industry standard is 10% of connections' prizemoney, which the trainer takes before distribution. Some trainers take more, especially if they are prominent and in demand. This should be disclosed in the PDS.

## 11. How is the horse insured?

While insurance is typically arranged by the syndicator, the trainer should be aware of the coverage and committed to maintaining it. Uninsured or underinsured horses are a risk that falls on owners.

## 12. Can you give me references from existing syndicate members?

A confident, reputable trainer will be happy to connect you with existing owner clients. Speaking to someone who has owned a horse with this trainer is one of the best pieces of due diligence you can do.
